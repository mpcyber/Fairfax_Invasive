Ailanthus altissima · Lycorma delicatula

Wherever Infrastructure Disturbs Soil, Tree of Heaven Follows

Fairfax County's invasive species pattern is not random. It is written by a century of linear infrastructure — and the next chapter is already under construction.

Act I — The Historical Seed
19th Century Railroad → W&OD Trail
Zone 1: Primary Hotspot

The county's largest A. altissima cluster traces directly to the former Washington & Old Dominion Railroad corridor. Rail corridors are among the most documented dispersal pathways for the species — disturbed ballast and embankments provided ideal colonization substrate, and populations established along the former line have been spreading laterally for over a century. The W&OD Trail, now a recreational path atop that right-of-way, runs through the heart of this hotspot.

Act II — The Modern Proof
Completed 2022
I-66 Transform Project

The largest road widening in recent Fairfax history tore up miles of roadside soil through the western corridor. Within two years, iNaturalist data shows a measurable density response along this axis — confirming that modern construction events replicate the same disturbance dynamic as the 19th-century railroad.

Active — Zone 2 Emerging
Route 28 / Sully Road

Ongoing widening through the Centreville–Chantilly corridor is already producing a secondary density cluster. At 2,290 Spotted Lanternfly sightings versus 640 Tree of Heaven records — a 3.6× ratio — the infestation is outpacing visible host plant detection.

Act III — The Predicted Wave
Under Construction Now
Richmond Highway — Embark Project

The Embark Richmond Highway initiative is actively rebuilding Route 1 through southeastern Fairfax — widening lanes, adding bus rapid transit, and relocating utilities across a 10-mile corridor. No significant A. altissima cluster exists here yet. Based on the pattern established by the W&OD corridor and I-66, the dashed zone on the map represents where one is likely to emerge.
